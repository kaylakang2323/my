In most organizations, role is treated as a primary unit of structure.

However, in practice, capability is not aligned one-to-one with role. It is unevenly distributed within the same role depending on individual experience, context, and organizational leverage.

Two people with identical titles may operate at significantly different levels of complexity. Conversely, the same individual may demonstrate different capability levels depending on domain, system maturity, and organizational clarity.

This creates a structural gap between formal role design and actual work execution.

Organizations typically resolve this gap informally through seniority, implicit expectations, or local knowledge. As a result, capability becomes visible only through performance signals, not through structural design.

The implication is not that roles are irrelevant. Roles remain necessary for coordination, accountability, and organizational clarity.

However, role alone is an incomplete descriptor of how work actually gets done.

High-performing organizations are not those that eliminate role distinctions, but those that better align capability signals with role structures—reducing the gap between formal design and operational reality.

In that sense, capability is not a replacement for role, but a hidden variable that determines how effectively a role is actually executed.
