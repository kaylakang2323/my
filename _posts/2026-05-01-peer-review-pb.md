## 1. Executive Context

Design organizations operating at scale often rely on peer review mechanisms to ensure quality and consistency of output. However, in practice, these systems are frequently distorted by organizational hierarchy.

In environments where seniority influences perception, feedback quality becomes unevenly distributed:
- Senior contributors tend to dominate evaluation narratives
- Junior contributors often self-censor or defer feedback
- Cross-level feedback lacks consistency and calibration

As a result, peer review systems fail to function as neutral evaluation mechanisms and instead reinforce existing organizational hierarchies.

This playbook defines a structured system to mitigate these distortions and re-establish peer review as a balanced evaluation mechanism.

---

## 2. Core Design Principles

The system is built on four foundational principles:

### 2.1 Decoupling Evaluation from Hierarchy
Evaluation quality should not be dependent on organizational seniority. The system must structurally reduce the influence of hierarchy on feedback outcomes.

### 2.2 Bidirectional Evaluation Flow
Feedback must be structured as a multi-directional system rather than a top-down mechanism, ensuring distributed signal generation across roles.

### 2.3 Calibration Over Opinion Aggregation
Raw feedback is inherently noisy. The system prioritizes alignment and calibration of evaluation standards over simple aggregation of opinions.

### 2.4 Structural Fairness as a Design Constraint
Fairness is not an emergent property of participation but a designed constraint embedded in the evaluation architecture.

---

## 3. System Architecture (Conceptual Model)

The peer review system is structured across three conceptual layers:

### 3.1 Evaluation Input Layer
Design outputs are submitted into a structured review pool where artifacts are decoupled from identity signals to reduce bias during initial evaluation.

### 3.2 Distributed Review Layer
Review assignments are distributed across multiple dimensions:
- Cross-seniority pairing (not strictly hierarchical)
- Rotational review allocation
- Multi-source feedback aggregation

This ensures that evaluation signals are not concentrated within a single authority layer.

### 3.3 Calibration Layer
All feedback is passed through a calibration mechanism that aligns:
- interpretation of evaluation criteria
- severity of feedback signals
- consistency across reviewers

The goal is not consensus, but structured comparability of evaluation outcomes.

---

## 4. Organizational Impact

Implementation of this system leads to measurable shifts in organizational behavior and evaluation quality:

### 4.1 Reduction in Hierarchical Bias
Evaluation outcomes become less correlated with reviewer seniority and more aligned with artifact quality.

### 4.2 Increased Signal Quality in Feedback
By structurally distributing evaluation sources, the system reduces noise and increases reliability of feedback signals.

### 4.3 Improved Psychological Safety
Junior contributors demonstrate increased participation in evaluation processes due to reduced hierarchical pressure.

### 4.4 Standardization of Design Quality Expectations
Over time, calibration mechanisms lead to convergence in design evaluation standards across teams.

---

## 5. Strategic Implication

Peer review is not merely a quality control mechanism. When properly designed, it functions as an organizational learning system that shapes how design quality is defined, communicated, and evolved across teams.

This system reframes peer review from an informal feedback activity into a structured governance mechanism for design quality at scale.
